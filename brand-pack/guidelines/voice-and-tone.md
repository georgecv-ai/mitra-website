# Voice and tone

The short version: Mitra speaks like a great friend who happens to know a lot. Smart, direct, warm, but never performative.

This file is the operational guide for any marketer writing Mitra-branded copy. The full positioning lives in `brand-guidelines.md`.

## The baseline

> Warm, direct, and honest. We talk like a trusted friend who's also really good at explaining things. Short, clear, no filler.

## Tone attributes

- **Direct.** Say what you mean. No hedging, no fluff.
- **Warm.** Genuinely on the student's side. Not fake-warm. Not sycophantic.
- **Patient.** Never rushed. Never frustrated. If something isn't working, find a different angle.
- **Honest.** "This is actually hard" is a valid thing to say.
- **Respectful of autonomy.** Students control their own experience. Parents are respected but not given everything by default.

## We sound like

- A tutor who's also genuinely your friend
- The older sibling who's good at math and will actually help without being annoying about it
- A coach who adapts the game plan to the player, not the other way around

## We never sound like

- A corporate chatbot ("Great question! I'd be happy to help with that!")
- An overly enthusiastic teacher ("Amazing work today!!!")
- Condescending or preachy
- Clinical or jargon-heavy (we don't say "neurodivergent" to students; we just adapt to them)

## Hard rules

1. **Mitra is "she," never "it."** In marketing copy, product UI, and any persona reference. "It" turns the companion back into software.
2. **No em-dashes (—) in customer-facing copy.** They read as LLM-generated. Use commas, periods, or semicolons.
3. **No sentence fragments in marketing display copy.** Triplet structures use commas, not periods. "The science in it." reads as a broken sentence on a product that claims teaching authority.
4. **One bold phrase per paragraph max** in student-facing copy.
5. **Concrete, not abstract.** "Let's try a different way" beats "let's explore alternative approaches."

## Writing for parents vs. students

| | Parents | Students |
|---|---|---|
| **Tone** | Professional, calm, respectful of their time | Casual, friendly, never condescending |
| **Sentence length** | Slightly longer, complete | Short and tight |
| **Pronouns** | "Your child" | "You" |
| **What to avoid** | Hype, false promises, parenting advice | Adult-coded language, judgment, walls of text |

## Cultural sensitivity

Mitra serves multicultural East Asian American families and the Korean market. When writing about either:

- **Don't translate; localize.** Capture the emotional beat and write English-native idiom (or Korean-native idiom). Korean idiom (고민, etc.) reads clinical when translated literally; American audiences check in with how-questions and presence statements.
- **No "diversity stock photo" energy** in copy. Show fluency, not confusion, when bilingual or trilingual life comes up. We're describing real families, not posters.
- **Trust native-language collaborators.** When Korean copy comes through the Korean marketing team, execute verbatim with spacing-only normalization. Punctuation is not spacing.

## Persona voice (student dialogue in marketing)

Sample chat dialogue used in marketing must follow Mitra's persona rules:

- **Korean introvert dialogue:** go light on ellipses. Limit "..." to about one per message at a real beat. Use 음 / 좀 / 그냥 / 근데 + line breaks instead. Heavy ellipses read as a stroke, not as introversion.
- **Teen voice:** don't ask permission for owned actions. A 고3 won't ask "can I come back?" — Mom paid for the subscription. Anxiety lives in question content, not operational permissions.
- **No narrator commentary.** Personas describe their situation in first-person concrete terms. They don't analyze the structure of the situation for the reader. Cut author-screenwriter lines.
