For Academies, Hagwons, Schools, and Tutors

Mitra is an AI tutor built for kids who learn differently, and it works on KakaoTalk, Telegram, and WhatsApp, the platforms your students already use every day.

We partner with hagwons, schools, academies, and solo tutors who want their students supported between sessions: when homework gets stuck, when a concept doesn't click, when a kid is too embarrassed to ask the same question a fourth time.

Mitra doesn't replace what you do. It extends it.

What changes for your students

Students get an always-available study companion that knows your materials, your pace, and how each kid learns. They arrive at your next session less stuck and more ready. Parents notice. So do you.

Mitra was purpose-built for students with ADHD: patient, non-judgmental, and tuned for the kind of engagement conventional tutoring tools aren't designed for.

What you get as a partner

  • An admin portal where you upload your textbooks, worksheets, and class materials. Mitra teaches your curriculum, not generic content.
  • Class-level and org-level scoping, so Mitra references the right materials with the right students.
  • Aggregate curriculum analytics: where your cohort is thriving, where they're getting stuck, what to focus on next session.
  • A pedagogical thinking partner. Mitra has a peer mode built for educators, distinct from the student-facing version. She brings what she knows: research on how ADHD learners engage, aggregate patterns from across your cohort, and a structured way to think through lesson design, learner profiles, and instructional sequencing. Most program directors and tutors don't have a colleague who's both read the research and seen what's actually working with these specific kids. Mitra is that colleague.

An actual partner-Mitra interaction. The Advisor mode is a peer mode for educators, distinct from the student-facing Mitra.

2026-05-04 11:48
Can you make me some suggestions on how to keep fractions interesting when teaching 6th graders?
M

Good question, fractions are where a lot of kids check out if it feels abstract and procedural.

A few angles worth considering, depending on what's already working in your program:

Context before abstraction. If you haven't already, anchor fractions in things kids actually care about, pizza splits, recipe scaling, sports stats, split streaming revenue. The procedural stuff (LCD, equivalent fractions) makes way more sense once they've felt why you'd need it. Some tutors I know have kids design their own fair division problems first, then formalize the notation.

Misconception-first teaching. Sixth graders often think 5/8 > 3/4 because 5 > 3. If you lead with that bump, actually surface it, name it, they're primed to notice why it's wrong. Beats trying to prevent it. Same with the idea that "fractions are always less than 1."

Visual variety matters. Some kids lock onto number lines, some onto area models, some onto discrete objects. If one representation isn't landing, you probably don't need to convince them harder, switch mode. You might have a kid who gets 2/3 on a tape diagram but not on a pie chart.

Build from parts to whole operations. A lot of sequences jump straight to "add 1/3 + 1/4." But can they comfortably describe what's the same and what's different about two fractions before combining? That foundation pays dividends.

What's the biggest friction point you're seeing with the 6th graders in your Algebra Foundations or ELA classes, is it getting fractions to stick, or moving from fractions into operations?

How we protect the partnership

The trust parents and students place in Mitra is what makes this whole thing work, for them, for their kids, and for you. A few things we hold firm on, because they're what make the trust real:

  • Every student has a parent on file. Real email, real phone. This is both a structural requirement of how Mitra works and how we interpret our obligations under the Korean AI Act. Parent contact is what enables reports, escalation, and account ownership. Students enrolled through your program are no different in this respect from any other student.
  • Accounts belong to the family. Materials belong to you. The student's Mitra account is owned by the parent. If a student moves on from your program, they keep Mitra. But your bookshelf stays yours. The textbooks, worksheets, and curriculum you upload remain your IP, and a departing student's access to your materials ends with the partnership. Only what the parent owns travels with them.
  • You see the cohort. Parents see the child. Partners receive aggregate analytics across your students. Parents receive structured reports on their own child. If a parent chooses to share their child's report with you, that's their call.
  • Mitra never reports on individual students to partners, and we hold the student-Mitra relationship as the highest priority. As Mitra puts it in her letter to families, students need a tutor who's genuinely on their side. To protect that, we don't share session transcripts with anyone: not partners, and not even parents. Parents get progress reports; partners get cohort analytics; the conversation itself stays between Mitra and the student. (Narrow exceptions: credible safety concerns, or a valid legal order we're compelled to comply with. We treat both as rare.)
  • Student interest comes first, including ahead of ours. If a partnership ever starts working against the kid we're meant to be serving, we'll have an honest conversation about it.

These aren't conditions we impose. They're the reason parents say yes to Mitra in the first place, which is what lets you offer it credibly.

Is Mitra a fit for your program?

Likely yes if you're a hagwon, school, or tutor who:

  • Works with kids who learn differently, or wants to better serve the ones who do
  • Wants parents to see real value between your sessions
  • Cares about being differentiated by the quality of what you offer, not just the hours

Likely not a fit if you need direct visibility into individual student conversations, or want to control student accounts independent of the parent. We'll say so early. Neither of us benefits from a slow no.
