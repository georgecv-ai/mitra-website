# Pain landing hero image briefs

One brief per pain. These feed VideoLab (or any image generator) to produce the
hero art that slots into `data/pains.json` under `hero_image`. Style anchor
applies to all 11 so the set reads as one system.

## Style anchor (applies to every brief)

- **Medium:** digital illustration, editorial style. Not flat cartoon. Not photo-real.
  Think New Yorker editorial illustration meets warm modern brand work.
- **Aspect:** 3:4 portrait (e.g. 1200x1600). Subject occupies upper-middle; breathe at bottom.
- **Mood:** observational, intimate, real. We're catching a moment, not staging a photo.
- **Lighting:** soft, directional, usually single-source (lamp, window, screen).
  Avoid flat studio light. Avoid neon. Avoid stark drama.
- **Palette anchor:** Mitra deep teal (#2A9D8F), cream/warm neutrals, coral accent (#E8736B).
  Each scene can pull 1-2 situational colors (lamp amber, screen blue, etc).
- **Characters:** realistic proportions, defined features (not photo-real). Ethnically
  representative — core audience is multicultural East Asian American families,
  but each pain can vary the kid's apparent background. Ages match the pain
  scenario (mostly 12-17).
- **Anti-cues across the board:**
  - No stock-photo perfect-family-smiling-at-camera
  - No literal product UI or logos (the page already shows that)
  - No corporate clip-art aesthetic
  - No saccharine "everything is great" energy when the pain is the point
  - Faces shown but not dominant — body language and scene carry the story

## 1. parent-stuck — *It's 8 p.m. Your kid is stuck. The tutor doesn't pick up.*

A parent at the kitchen table next to their middle-schooler. Textbook open,
worksheet half done. The parent is mid-thought, looking at the homework with
a flicker of "I don't remember this" recognition. Their hand is on or reaching
for their phone — the moment of realizing they need outside help. The child
has head in hand or stares blankly at the page. Warm overhead lamp light,
mug still on the table from dinner, slight real-home clutter.
**Palette accent:** warm amber lamp tones, faint teal phone glow.
**Avoid:** the parent looking encouraging at the child. This is the stuck
moment, not the helping moment.

## 2. tutor-cost — *A month of tutoring. Grades still flat.*

A parent at a desk or kitchen surface looking at evidence of money spent
without payoff. On screen or in front of them: a calendar dense with marked
tutoring sessions, or a receipt stack. Nearby: a report card showing flat
numbers. The parent's posture says "this isn't working." A mug or glass
suggests it's late evening, alone with the math. Soft desk-lamp lighting.
**Palette accent:** cool paper-whites and gray-blues with one warm lamp pool.
**Avoid:** literal dollar bills or cash. Show the receipt/calendar abstraction.
Avoid an angry parent. This is tired, not furious.

## 3. home-alone — *House quiet. Page blank. Nobody to ask.*

A teenager alone at their desk at night. Page genuinely blank in front of
them. Phone face-up next to them, glowing faintly. The room is mostly dark,
one desk-lamp pool of light around the work surface. Through an open doorway,
a hallway dark, parent's room dark. Kid's chin in hand, mid-thought, quietly
stuck — not crying, not despairing.
**Palette accent:** deep navy room, warm desk-lamp pool, teal phone glow.
**Avoid:** laptop or TV light. The lamp + phone is the only light source.
Avoid tears or visible distress. This is quiet, not crisis.

## 4. short-time — *Twelve minutes free. Not enough for a tutor. Plenty for Mitra.*

A kid mid-transition between activities. Sports bag dropped by the door,
uniform half-changed or jacket still on. They're perched on the kitchen
counter or floor, phone in hand mid-chat, snack half-eaten beside them.
A clock somewhere in frame (wall, microwave, phone lock screen). Backpack
open, schoolwork visible. Real "between things" energy.
**Palette accent:** bright daylight or kitchen warmth, lots of activity color.
**Avoid:** stressed or panicked body language. This is a kid using found
minutes well, not a kid drowning. No anxiety.

## 5. not-interested — *He doesn't care about fractions. He cares about Minecraft.*

A boy around 13 mid-Minecraft (or stylized blocky game) session on a desktop
or tablet, totally absorbed. Schoolbooks are visible in the frame — but
actively shoved aside, not stacked neatly. The composition makes the school
stuff peripheral and the game central. He's leaning into the screen, eyes
locked. Late-afternoon room light.
**Palette accent:** green/yellow game glow lighting his face, room muted around it.
**Avoid:** a parent in the frame looking disappointed — this is the kid's POV,
not the parent's judgment. Don't make him look lazy. He's engaged, just in
the wrong thing.

## 6. grades-miss — *Her teacher says she's not paying attention. At home, it's quantum physics.*

A girl ~14-15 in her bedroom or at the kitchen table, actively explaining
something to someone off-frame (or thinking out loud). Hands gesture
mid-explanation. Around her: a notebook with her own diagrams (atoms, waves,
equations) — clearly self-driven, not assigned. Mug of tea, earbuds around
neck. Posture is animated, engaged. The opposite of "not paying attention."
**Palette accent:** warm daylight or evening lamp, deep teal notebook spine.
**Avoid:** classroom setting. This is HOME mode. Avoid having her look at a
screen — show her thinking and explaining, not consuming.

## 7. wont-ask — *I'd rather get it wrong than raise my hand.*

A shy student in a classroom mid-lesson. Around her, several classmates'
hands are up — visible but soft-focus. Her hand is firmly down, on her desk
or in her lap. Her eyes are on her paper, deliberately not looking at the
teacher. Body language: shrunk in, not slouched but contained. Teacher
visible in soft focus toward the front.
**Palette accent:** cool classroom palette with one warm accent on her
(hoodie, notebook, sleeve).
**Avoid:** tears or distress. This is everyday quiet retreat, not crisis.
Don't show classmates mocking — the silence around her IS the point.

## 8. needs-patience — *Some kids need ten tries to start. Mitra never sighs.*

A child (could be a bit younger, 10-12) at a desk or kitchen table, head
down on folded arms, paper in front of them barely touched. From the side,
an adult hand visible — placing a mug of tea gently next to them, no
pressure. The scene reads: "stuck at the starting line, with someone
alongside who is not rushing them."
**Palette accent:** warm wood tones, soft cream paper, deep teal mug.
**Avoid:** showing the adult's face. The patient HAND is the whole point.
Avoid the child crying. This is the quiet stuck-ness of executive
dysfunction, not emotional breakdown.

## 9. past-tutors — *He's past you. Past his tutors. Still asking why.*

A teenage boy ~15-17 in a study space surrounded by advanced material:
university-level textbooks, dense equations on loose paper, a whiteboard or
wall with derivations he wrote himself. His face is mid-question, looking
up from the work — searching for someone who can match the depth. The room
is empty around him. A closed laptop nearby implies "the AI couldn't keep up
either." Soft late-afternoon window light.
**Palette accent:** warm wood, cream paper, deep teal book spines.
**Avoid:** making him look arrogant or aloof. This is curiosity unmet, not
condescension. No tutor figure in the frame.

## 10. not-answer-bot — *The AI that hands the answer over makes your child weaker.*

Dual-element composition implying contrast. Foreground: a slumped, disengaged
kid copying an answer off a phone or laptop — eyes glazed, no thinking
happening, hand mechanically writing. In a softer second register (background
panel, or below, or a thought-bubble framing): the same kid working through
a problem actively, papers around them, mid-thought, alive. The contrast IS
the brief.
**Palette accent:** cooler desaturated tones on the answer-copying side;
warmer engaged tones on the working-it-out side.
**Avoid:** making the AI device look dystopian or evil. It's quiet harm, not
horror. No sad parent in frame — this is the kid's outcome we're showing.

## 11. multilingual — *Math homework in English. Grandma on the phone in Korean. Spanish at the dinner table.*

A teenage girl in a home space at the intersection of three languages.
Beside her: an English math textbook open. In her hand: phone with a Korean
chat thread (or 한국어 bubbles abstracted). Background, soft-focus: family
dinner table where Spanish conversation is happening (a parent, grandparent,
or sibling). She's comfortable at the intersection, not stressed. Visual
cues for each culture: a Korean ceramic on a shelf, Spanish cookware on the
counter, English notebook on the desk.
**Palette accent:** warm family-home palette with multiple cultural color
notes (a kimchi-jar red, a saffron yellow, a denim blue).
**Avoid:** forced "diversity stock photo" energy. This is ONE family, not a
UN poster. Show fluency, not confusion. No stressed expressions.
